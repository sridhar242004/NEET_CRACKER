Dark Reactions (Calvin Cycle)

Learn about the biochemical pathway plants use to convert carbon dioxide into organic molecules during photosynthesis.

Overview of the Calvin Cycle

The Calvin Cycle, also known as the dark reactions, occurs in the stroma of chloroplasts where it uses energy from ATP and NADPH (produced in the light reactions) to fix carbon dioxide and form glucose.

Carbon Fixation

The enzyme Rubisco helps fix CO₂ by attaching it to a 5-carbon molecule, ribulose bisphosphate (RuBP), forming an unstable 6-carbon compound that splits into two 3-carbon molecules.

Reduction Phase

Using ATP and NADPH, the 3-carbon molecules are converted into glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), a 3-carbon sugar that can be used to form glucose.

Regeneration of RuBP

Some of the G3P molecules are used to regenerate RuBP, allowing the cycle to continue. This step also uses ATP.

Importance of Calvin Cycle

The Calvin Cycle is essential for the synthesis of glucose and other organic molecules necessary for plant growth and energy storage.

Key Steps and Molecules Involved

Each phase in the Calvin Cycle involves specific molecules and reactions, contributing to the formation of stable organic compounds.

ATP and NADPH

Energy carriers produced in the light reactions that provide energy and reducing power for the Calvin Cycle.

Glyceraldehyde-3-Phosphate (G3P)

A 3-carbon molecule produced in the reduction phase, which is a building block for glucose and other carbohydrates.

Rubisco Enzyme

The key enzyme that catalyzes the first step of the Calvin Cycle by fixing CO₂ into an organic molecule.

RuBP (Ribulose Bisphosphate)

A 5-carbon molecule that combines with CO₂ to initiate the cycle, allowing it to operate in a continuous loop.
